What are Invasive Species?

An invasive species is a non-native organism that is introduced to an ecosystem and causes harm to the environment, economy, or human health. They outcompete native species, disrupt ecological processes, and harm biodiversity. Invasive species can be plants, animals, or microorganisms, and are often introduced through human activities. Once established, they rapidly multiply, spread, and negatively impact natural habitats. Prevention, early detection, and control efforts are important to protect native ecosystems and preserve biodiversity.

European Starling

Sturnus vulgaris

The European starling (Sturnus vulgaris) is a medium-sized songbird belonging to the family Sturnidae.
Appearance: European starlings have a compact and stocky body, measuring around 20 centimeters (8 inches) in length. They have a short tail and a pointed, yellow bill. In their breeding plumage, adults have glossy black feathers with iridescent green and purple tones. During the winter months, their plumage becomes speckled with white spots. Their legs are pinkish or reddish in color. Juvenile starlings have duller plumage and lack the distinctive iridescence of adults.
Behavior: European starlings are highly gregarious birds that often form large flocks, particularly during the non-breeding season. They are known for their aerial acrobatics, flying in tight formations and performing synchronized, swirling patterns known as murmurations. Starlings are excellent mimics and can imitate various sounds and calls, including human speech. They are omnivorous, feeding on a wide range of foods, including insects, fruits, berries, seeds, and scraps of human food.
Distribution and Native Range: The European starling is native to Europe, western Asia, and northern Africa. However, it has been introduced to various parts of the world, including North America, South America, Australia, and New Zealand. The introduction of starlings was intentional in some cases, while in others, they were introduced to control insect pests or for ornamental purposes. European starlings are adaptable birds and can thrive in a variety of habitats, including urban areas, farmlands, woodlands, and open grasslands.

House Sparrow

Passer Domesticus

The house sparrow (Passer domesticus) is a small bird belonging to the family Passeridae. It is also known simply as the sparrow or English sparrow.
Appearance: House sparrows have a compact body measuring around 14 to 16 centimeters (5.5 to 6.3 inches) in length. They have a stout build with rounded wings and a short, thick bill. Males and females have different plumage. Adult males typically have a gray crown, black throat, and white cheeks, along with a chestnut-brown back and wings. Their underparts are pale gray. Females and juveniles are duller in coloration, with streaked brown plumage and a pale supercilium (eyebrow stripe).
Behavior: House sparrows are highly social birds that often gather in large flocks. They are opportunistic feeders, consuming a variety of seeds, grains, insects, and scraps of food. They are adaptable and can thrive in urban, suburban, and rural environments. House sparrows are known for their chirping and chattering vocalizations, which are often heard as they communicate with each other.
Distribution and Native Range: The house sparrow is not native to North America. It is believed to have originated in the Middle East, specifically in the region spanning from Iraq to Iran. However, it has been widely introduced and naturalized throughout the world, including North America, Europe, Asia, Africa, and various oceanic islands. The spread of house sparrows has been facilitated by human activities, such as the provision of food and nest sites in urban areas.

Feral Pigeon

Columba Livia Domestica

The feral pigeon, also known as the rock pigeon or city pigeon, is a bird that belongs to the Columbidae family.
Appearance: Feral pigeons vary in coloration, but most have a grayish body with a lighter underside. They have a small head, short neck, and a plump body. The wings are broad and rounded, allowing for agile flight. Feral pigeons have a distinct iridescent patch on their necks, which can display various shades of green, purple, or bronze. Their eyes have a reddish hue, and they possess a strong beak.
Behavior: Feral pigeons are highly adaptable and are commonly found in urban and suburban areas around the world. They are social birds that often form large flocks. Pigeons are known for their cooing calls and are capable of various vocalizations. They are excellent flyers and can maneuver through cities and buildings with ease. Pigeons are primarily ground feeders, consuming a wide variety of grains, seeds, and scraps of food.
Distribution and Native Range: The feral pigeon is not a distinct species but rather a domesticated form of the rock pigeon (Columba livia). Rock pigeons are native to Europe, North Africa, and southwestern Asia. However, through domestication and subsequent escapes or releases, feral pigeon populations can now be found worldwide. They are especially abundant in urban environments, where they have adapted to nesting on buildings, bridges, and other structures.

Red-Whiskered Bulbul

Pycnonotus Jocosus

The Red-whiskered bulbul (Pycnonotus jocosus) is a medium-sized songbird belonging to the family Pycnonotidae.
Appearance: Red-whiskered bulbuls have a slender body, measuring about 20 centimeters (8 inches) in length. They have a distinctive appearance with a black crest on their head, which is often raised when they are excited or alarmed. The upperparts of their body are olive-green, while their underparts are off-white or pale yellow. One of their most notable features is the red patch of feathers on the side of their face, extending from the base of the bill to behind the eye. They also have a thin black line extending from the eye to the back of the head.
Behavior: Red-whiskered bulbuls are active and agile birds, often seen hopping and flitting between branches in trees. They are known for their melodious songs, consisting of a variety of rich and varied notes, including whistles, trills, and warbles. They are omnivorous and feed on a diverse diet, including fruits, berries, nectar, insects, and small invertebrates.
Distribution and Native Range: The Red-whiskered bulbul is native to southern Asia. Its natural range extends from India and Sri Lanka through Southeast Asia, including Myanmar, Thailand, Vietnam, and Indonesia. It has also been introduced to other parts of the world, including Australia, Fiji, Mauritius, and Hawaii. Red-whiskered bulbuls are adaptable birds that thrive in a variety of habitats, including gardens, parks, forests, and agricultural areas.

Indian Peacock

Pavo Cristatus

The Indian peacock, also known as the Indian peafowl, is a large and colorful bird belonging to the Phasianidae family.
Appearance: The male Indian peacock is renowned for its extravagant plumage. It has a long and elegant train consisting of elongated, brilliantly colored feathers. These feathers are iridescent, displaying shimmering shades of blue, green, and gold. The train feathers have intricate eye-spots at the tips, which are known as "ocelli." The peacock's body is compact and covered in dark blue feathers. Its head is adorned with a crest of feathers that can be raised and lowered. The female Indian peafowl, known as a peahen, has a more subdued appearance. It has a brownish overall coloration with some hints of green and lacks the ornate train.
Behavior: Indian peafowls are primarily ground-dwelling birds. The males use their beautiful trains for courtship displays. During mating season, the males fan out their trains and perform elaborate dances, accompanied by vocalizations and shaking of the feathers. These displays are intended to attract the attention of females. Peafowls are omnivorous and feed on a variety of foods, including grains, fruits, insects, and small reptiles. They are typically found in forested areas, agricultural lands, and open grasslands.
Distribution and Native Range: The Indian peacock is native to the Indian subcontinent, including India, Sri Lanka, Nepal, Bhutan, and Bangladesh. It is also the national bird of India. However, due to their captivating beauty, Indian peafowls have been introduced to various parts of the world, including Europe, North America, and Australia. In some regions, they have established feral populations.

Muscovy Duck

Cairina Moschata

The Muscovy duck (Cairina moschata) is a large waterfowl species belonging to the family Anatidae.
Appearance: Muscovy ducks are relatively large ducks, with males typically weighing between 3.5 to 6 kilograms (7.7 to 13.2 pounds) and females weighing slightly less. They have a distinctive appearance with a long, slender body and a flat, wide bill. Males are larger than females and have more pronounced caruncles, which are fleshy, red, and wart-like growths on their face and neck. Muscovy ducks exhibit significant sexual dimorphism. Males have a blackish plumage with iridescent green or purple gloss, while females have a mottled brownish-black coloration with white patches on the wings.
Behavior: Muscovy ducks are generally less social compared to other duck species and often form small family groups. They are excellent swimmers and have sharp claws on their webbed feet, which enable them to perch in trees. They are adaptable birds, found in a range of habitats, including lakes, ponds, rivers, and wetlands. Muscovy ducks are known to be highly territorial and may aggressively defend their nesting sites.
Distribution and Native Range: The Muscovy duck is native to parts of Central and South America, including Mexico, Central America, and northern South America. However, they have been domesticated and introduced to various parts of the world, including North America, Europe, and Asia. In some regions, Muscovy ducks have established feral populations. They are particularly common in urban and suburban areas with access to water bodies.
